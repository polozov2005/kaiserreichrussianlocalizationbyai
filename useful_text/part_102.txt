Initial Land Reform
The Land Reform Act is designed to limit landlord's power and provide arable land to poor farmers. Our initial plans will enforce redistribution of all land owned by absentee landlords within the next four years.
Continued Land Reform
The expanded version of the Land Reform Act will redistribute land that exceeds the newly defined limits of individual ownership, and give the landlords state bonds equal to the purchase price of their lost property as compensation. This will be a major step towards ending the rural poverty that has plagued our nation for generations.
Extensive Land Reform
The most extensive version of the Land Reform Act gives extended powers to government officials to fight those who resist these reforms.
Land Readjustment Act
The Land Adjustment Act will establish a legal basis for tenant rights. The Act will prohibit landlords from cancelling or refusing to renew a contract unilaterally without due cause.
Rent Control Act
The Rent Control Act will allow local authorities to implement rent controls and authorise prefecture governors to intervene in cases where the rent has been proven to be unreasonably high.
Agricultural Land Control Act
The Agricultural Land Control Act stipulates that conversion or trade of farmland for non-agricultural purposes must be approved by local authorities. This allows us to select what crops are planted, in order to secure high production of staple foods.

The plight was particularly severe in the agricultural sector and caused a massive demographic change, as farmers fled from the depressed countryside to urban areas, flooding the labour market, and further driving down wages.\n\nAfter leaving the Kenseikai-led 'Constitutionalist Coalition' and taking charge of the Government, Inukai and his cabinet successfully expanded the Japanese sphere of influence into Manchuria and the edges of Siberia, acquiring a vital export market for the Japanese economy, but the agricultural situation continued to deteriorate as cheap agricultural imports from Manchuria bled into the countryside. As 1936 dawns, continued rearmament and state investment have fully rebuilt the industrial economy, but the issue of rural poverty remains chronic and unsolved.

Hirohito
§YBorn:§! §LApril 29th, 1901, in Tōgū Palace, Aoyama, Tōkyō Prefecture, Empire of Japan§!\n§YBackground:§! §L$JAP_hirohito_desc_body$§!
Hirohito, the 124th Emperor of Japan, ascended to the Chrysanthemum Throne in 1926 following the death of his father, Emperor Taishō. Having served as regent from 1921, the young prince was confronted with several disasters and crises, including the Great Kantō Earthquake and an assassination attempt, both in 1923. Now, having been Emperor for a decade, during which he largely remained aloof from the parliamentary politics that took hold during his regency, he maintains the role of a spiritual leader of the nation, the divine embodiment of Japan.\n\nThe Emperor himself is an enigmatic and even indecisive man, holding considerable power in Japanese politics but in practice often relying on the advice of his Privy Council. He is eager and passive by turns, determined to maintain a strong role in the running of his country but often acquiescing to the wishes of his advisors or the military. His political evolution has led him to become a staunch supporter of Japan's imperial ambitions, and he has resolved to play what he sees as his necessary role in attaining imperial glory for Japan.
It would appear that harsh and uncertain times have befallen the once formerly great Japanese power. Once regarded as the strongest naval power in all of East Asia, one worthy of competing with the Kaiserliche Marine, the Japanese Empire's daring ambition would soon prove to be their downfall. After a victorious land and sea campaign by their enemy forces, Japan has become completely occupied for the first time in its history.

Miyazaki Ryusuke
§YBorn:§! §LNovember 2nd, 1892, in Arao Village, Tamana District, Kumamoto Prefecture, Empire of Japan§!\n§YBackground:§! §LThe eldest son of Miyazaki Toten, a Japanese political activist and supporter of Chinese revolutionary Sun Yat-sen, the younger Miyazaki entered the Tokyo Imperial University School of Law in 1916 where he got his own start in politics promoting Taisho democracy with the Shinjinkai (New Members Association) along with Akamatsu Katsumaro and Ishiwata Haruo and also met Yoshino Sakuza. Ryusuke eloped with Yanagiwara Byakuren, a well-known poet and wife of a coal mining magnate, triggering a major scandal.\n\nAfterwards, he worked as a lawyer under Katayama Satoshi and Hoshijima Jiro but became ill with tuberculosis. After spending a few years recovering, he founded the Independent Labor Association with Yoshino Sakuzo and Abe Isoo in 1926 and then became a central committee member of the Social Democratic Party. He became a minor politician, running unsuccessfully for political office and associating with other Pan-Asian politicians - even taking a right-wing turn to ally with Nakano Seigo.\n\nHe would mostly be forgotten by history, if it was not for his role as an intermediary between the Japanese government and the ascendant Kuomintang. Sent by the civilian government as a secret emissary to try and defuse tensions with the party-state, he was detained by the displeased Japanese military before he could leave Japan. However, with the KMT accomplishing the impossible and landing in Japan itself, Ryusuke was sent as part of a last ditch negotiating team and ultimately selected by Chinese occupation authorities to oversee Japan's transition into a sister republic.§!
