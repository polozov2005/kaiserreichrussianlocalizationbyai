Building a Chinese Domestic Defence Industry
One of the few silver linings of decades of civil war is that heavy military spending has built a sturdy foundation for a modern military-industrial complex. However, many barriers remain. Besides the usual suspects of corruption, inexperience and wartime disruption, Chinese soldiers historically have been equipped with foreign designs, if not outright foreign imports, stifling the growth of a domestic arms industry. Furthermore, most of China's vast arsenals are heavily decentralised and poorly integrated, a product of warlord regionalism. As we march towards unification, we must work to capture and synchronise these military production hubs, unleashing the full potential of the Chinese industrial behemoth!

Shenyang Arsenal is perhaps one of the hallmark achievements of the Fengtian clique, created and maintained with the help of Japanese advisors to maintain Zhang Zuolin's grip over Manchuria and fuel his ambitions of uniting China under his banner. Formally known as the Eastern Three Province Arsenal, it is one of the largest in China and boasts of its ability to produce more arms than all others in China combined across the 1920s. Taking advantage of Dongbei's superior industrial might and rich natural resources, Shenyang Arsenal became a centre for not only production but also creating an extensive military logistical chain (including centralised production of rations and uniforms), as well as weapons development of their own. While Zhang was unable to fully break China's dependence on foreign arms imports, perhaps under our management it will play a valuable role in building a domestic arms industry.

Integrate Jinan Arsenal
Created as the Shandong Machinery Bureau by Governor Ding Baozhen in 1875, it acquired machinery needed to produce weapons a few years later in 1883. It began producing a series of light arms derived from western designs, quickly expanding its industrial base to producing increasingly large amounts of rifles and other infantry equipment. Renamed Shandong Arsenal by the Beiyang Government, it saw further production and its final name as Jinan Arsenal under Zhang Zhongchang's rule.

Integrate Guizhou Arsenal
The Guizhou Arsenal was founded in August 1926 in Guiyang by Zhou Xichen, through the merger of the Chishui Arsenal and the Yanhe Gun Factory, which were initially established by Zhou in 1923 and 1924 respectively, to manufacture pistols and rifles for his power struggle in Guizhou. Joined by a smokeless powder factory which greatly bolstered production, the Guizhou Arsenal now produced machine guns, grenades, shells and even mines for Zhou Xichen's armies.

The port of Yingkou sits in a strategic site in the Yellow Sea, positioned in a controlling position along the Daliao river and Liaodong Bay. With Dalian (Port Arthur) under Japanese control, Yingkou was effectively the sole port under the Fengtian clique's direct control and significant (albeit insufficient) funding was provided to expand it as the home of their fleet. Integrate Jiangnan Shipyards
Jiangnan Shipyard was founded in 1865 to build, repair, and covert both military and civilian ships alike. Located near the Yangtze and situated largely within the mighty port of Shanghai, Jiangnan is one of the premier shipbuilding centres in all of China. Long strongly under the control of the Beiyang government, the creation of the Legation Cities altered the dynamic significantly. Still, they have benefited from outside investment and expertise, something we must bring under our control for a more equal future.
Integrate the Fuzhou Arsenal
Fuzhou Arsenal, also known as the Foochow Arsenal or Mawei Arsenal, was created under the orders of late Qing Dynasty leaders Li Hongzhang and Zuo Zongtang a few miles up the Min River. Taking advantage of Fujian's proud naval heritage and local naval academies, the Fuzhou arsenal complimented the region by providing a western designed naval dockyard, a metal-working forge, and additional schools for navigation and marine engineering. Damaged in ensuing wars, it was rebuilt and remained a prominent dockyard though under Legation control. Having secured its return, we must utilise it to its fullest capacity in helping us build a modern navy.
Integrate Whampoa Dock Company
The Hong Kong and Whampoa Dock Company was founded by Douglas Lapraik and Thomas Sutherland in 1866. Often abbreviated Whampoa Dock or Kowloon Docks, it effectively monopolised shipbuilding in the Pearl River Delta across the 1870s. The Canton region has traditionally been a centre for external trade, and its location made it both an asset and a target in the South China sea. We shall integrate these newly acquired facilities into our navy.
Integrate Dagu Shipyard
Established in 1880 by Li Hongzhang in Dagu, the Dagu Shipyard was initially tasked with only repairing ships for the Beiyang Fleet. Two years later, it began producing warships and in 1891 it even produced firearms like naval guns. Occupied in 1900 by the powers and returned to China two years later, the Dagu Shipyard remained prominent in the Warlord Era. It also was notable for producing European designed machine guns for the Ministry of Navy.
