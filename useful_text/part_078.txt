Zhang Zuolin
Born to an impoverished family near Shenyang, Zhang Zuolin began his meteoric rise as a bandit exploiting the chaotic military situation of the late Guangxu Era. In the aftermath of the Xinhai Revolution, Zhang was able to secure control of his home province of Fengtian. Zhang's large assortment of alliances, composed of local Beiyang officers and old bandit friends, formed the nucleus of the Fengtian Clique, which would become one of the main Beiyang Clique splinters.\n\nZhang originally made an alliance with Duan Qirui to utilise his Japanese connections for the purpose of securing firearms, but turned on the Anhui Clique alongside the Zhili Clique. The two warlord cliques would later emerge as arch-rivals, a state of affairs still present today. For a time Zhang was the sole controller of Beijing, but the Zhili Clique would restore their control over the city in 1927.\n\nIn the aftermath of the Qing Restoration, Zhang carried out the independence of the Northeast from Beijing's authority and challenged their direct authority to rule China, claiming for himself the mantle of the Xinhai Revolution and rightful heir of the Beiyang Government. Zhang's true commitment to democratic republicanism is dubious at best, but if the Old Marshal can utilise republican rhetoric to restore his status as the internationally recognised government, then so be it.

Zhang Xueliang
Zhang Xueliang was born into the opposite conditions when compared to his father, the Old Marshal. While Zhang Zuolin was born into poverty and had no formal education, he ensured that his son would be born into a life of privilege and had access to private tutors. Zhang Xueliang would grow up much more open to Western ideas than his conservative father who was disliked by China's intellectual class. Seeking to follow in his father's footsteps, Zhang enrolled in the Fengtian Lecture Hall.\n\nAfter graduating from the artillery department, Zhang Zuolin promoted his son to brigade commander of the guards brigade of the Northeast. When the Fengtian Clique was defeated by the Zhili Clique in the 1st Zhifeng War, Zhang Xueliang was tasked by his father to negotiate a peace. In order to avenge his defeat, Zhang Zuolin began a great modernisation effort in his armies, and Zhang Xueliang was made supervisor of the Northeast Aviation Office, tasked with delivering his father a modern air force which he achieved via the purchase of foreign planes.\n\nThe efforts of the 'Young Marshal' paid off in the 2nd Zhifeng War, which saw the Fengtian Clique secure control of Beijing and dominate Chinese politics.

Feng Yong
A dear friend of Zhang Xueliang, having been close since childhood, Feng Yong is the eldest son of Feng Delin who was one of the old veterans of the Fengtian Clique, and an ally of Zhang Zuolin. Sharing the same courtesy name as Xueliang, and growing up together, Feng would accompany Xueliang to the Northeast Army Lecture Hall, and similarly to the Baoding Army Officers School. Serving as a staff officer of the Fengtian Aviation Department, he would play a key role in the establishment of the Northeast Air Force.\n\nFollowing the First ZhiFeng War in 1922, Feng would grow disgusted with the concept of war, resigning from the military and became the general manager of Daye Iron Factory, engaging in armament production, and concurrently principal of Daye Factory Engineer School. In 1926 he donated 3.1 million yuan to establish Feng Yong University, serving as its principal — the first Western style university in the Northeast. With free tuition, and the attaching of great importance to military and physical education, his ideas had considerable effect on the Northeast's students.\n\nFollowing the onset of the Fourth and Fifth ZhiFeng Wars, Feng would emerge out of retirement at the behest of Xueliang, but not to fight the Zhili. Organising his own forces, Feng would focus instead on blunting Japanese operations within Manchuria, allegedly on the Old Marshal's consent.

Receiving a standard education, Ruan Zhenduo would graduate from the Japanese styled Nanman Medical College in 1918 after a series of stints in lower educational institutions. Graduating with a medical degree, Ruan would serve as a physician at Fengtian Mantie Hospital, and temporarily served concurrently as a plague prevention physician in Shenyang. From 1919 to 1920, Ruan would study medical chemistry at the School of Medicine in Kyoto Imperial University, and would return to China in 1921 to serve as a school official, and school physician at Northeastern University.\n\nServing as a doctor up until 1924, he would become president of the Fengtian branch of the All-China Medical Association, and in 1928 would be appointed as the Assistant Officer for Plague Prevention. In 1930, Ruan would serve as director of the Changchun Ji-Chang Ji-Dun Railway Hospital, and in the following year he would be appointed as the consultant of the Fengtian Local Maintenance Association.\n\nShooting into politics utilising Japanese contacts, Ruan would be appointed as Secretary-General of the Fengtian Provincial Government, and would establish the Concordia Association, with others from the Manchuria Youth League, in order to be the facet by which Japan influences Fengtian's Assembly. Continuing to dutifully display his expertise in a series of transportaion and communication positions, Ruan would continue to champion the cause of Japan in the rump assembly.

The Moral Republic
Establishing Republican Legitimacy
As a nation, China appears to have utterly lost itself; its national character, the very structure by which families are brought together, and by which society prospers. Abounding ever since November 4th, there has been a great recoil from the merits of Confucianism, yet equally Neo-Confucianism has brough forward a rejuvenated strain of the all-encompassing national philosophy, and such traditions must be respected.
The notion of a Chinese Republic has seen itself muddied, or otherwise confused over the long decades of the Republican Experiment. Unable to truly grasp its true purpose, and sometimes even briefly fading back into autocratic despotism, the Republic is in dire need of true legitimacy; one untainted by mismanagement and kleptocracy, and fueled by genuine intent for a strong and resilient nation-state.
Rulership as a Family Business
Reign of the Meritorious
Unsuited to the presidency, either out of apathy, desire to remain close to military matters, or insecurities considering his reputation within China, the Old Marshal has deemed it fit finally for the Young Marshal to take the reigns, with his father's careful guidance of course. Young and open to reformism, the Republic's father and son look forward onto a bright future for China.
Unlike the dastardly insular Zhang family, Yang Yuting, Chang Yinhuai, Yu Zhen, and Yi Jiqiao understand that China cannot be ruled by familial ties any longer; Cao Kun proved it at its worse, and on the precipice, Zhang Zuolin was likely to follow. The nation must be governed by its most skilled, its technocrats, and its reliable stewards, for the nation must advance beyond nepotism.
Denouncing Imperialist Vultures
Rationalisation of Class and Society
China has long been regarded as a second-rate power, a playground for the Great Powers to meddle and feud over; if it wasn't the British, it was the Russians, or the Germans. And even now, in this era far detached from the heyday of colonialism, the Great Powers continue to maintain their control in key concessions throughout the country; how can such a disaster be permitted in this new era?
Whilst perhaps enemies in any situation other than that of Yang Yuting's government, the Young China Party and the Concordia Association have miraculously found common ground. Desiring a new nation, governed by corporatism and of an all-inclusive national whole, they have buttressed Yuting's government in a form upon which all classes of China, and all stratums, shall work towards national progress.
